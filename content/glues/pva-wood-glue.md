# PVA Wood Glue: What It's Good For, and How to Get the Most Out of It

If you've ever built furniture, PVA is probably the glue you reached for without thinking about it. It's the yellow or white stuff in nearly every workshop, and it earns that spot honestly.

## Fun Facts & Trivia

- PVA stands for polyvinyl acetate, a synthetic polymer that was already in industrial use by the 1930s before woodworkers adopted it.
- A properly clamped, properly fitted PVA joint is often stronger than the wood around it — break-test a scrap joint and you'll usually see the wood fibers tear before the glue line lets go.
- PVA cures by losing water into the surrounding wood and air, not through a chemical reaction — which is why clamping pressure and a snug joint matter so much more than with epoxy.

## Best Uses

- Wood-to-wood joints: furniture, cabinetry, trim work
- Porous crafts: paper, cork, unfinished wood
- Anywhere you can clamp for at least 30 minutes

## Tips for a Bond That Lasts

- Spread a thin, even layer on both faces — a thick glob just squeezes out and weakens the joint.
- Clamp firmly but don't overtighten; starving the joint of glue is as bad as too much.
- Wipe squeeze-out with a damp cloth immediately — dried PVA laughs at sandpaper.
- Don't rely on it outdoors unless the label specifically says water-resistant (Type II/III).
