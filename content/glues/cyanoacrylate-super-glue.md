# Cyanoacrylate (Super Glue): What It's Good For, and How to Get the Most Out of It

Super glue feels like magic — a drop, a few seconds of pressure, and it's done. The chemistry behind that speed is stranger than most people realize.

## Fun Facts & Trivia

- Cyanoacrylate was discovered by accident in 1942 by researcher Harry Coover, who was actually trying to develop clear plastic gun sights — the compound kept gluing his lab equipment together instead.
- It doesn't cure by drying out — it cures by reacting with trace moisture already present on almost every surface, including your skin, which is exactly why it bonds fingers together so fast.
- During the Vietnam War, a spray version of cyanoacrylate was used in the field to close wounds and stop bleeding before a soldier could reach surgery.

## Best Uses

- Small, tight-fitting breaks in ceramics, glass, and most rigid plastics
- Quick repairs where clamping isn't practical
- Precision work where a thin bond line matters

## Tips for a Bond That Lasts

- It needs a snug, gap-free fit — cyanoacrylate is not a gap-filler, switch to epoxy if the pieces don't sit flush.
- Gel formulas are worth the extra cost on vertical or uneven surfaces where liquid CA just runs off.
- It will not bond polypropylene or polyethylene plastics — check the plastic type before you blame the glue.
- Keep acetone nearby for cleanup and, if needed, for un-gluing fingers.
