# Contact Cement: What It's Good For, and How to Get the Most Out of It

Contact cement breaks the normal rules of gluing: you don't join wet surfaces, you let the glue dry first, then press the pieces together and it bonds instantly. That's not a workaround — it's the whole point.

## Fun Facts & Trivia

- Its neoprene-rubber base was originally developed for bonding rubber and was later adopted heavily by the footwear industry for attaching soles to uppers.
- Because the bond forms the instant two dry, tacky surfaces touch, there's essentially no repositioning window — pros use spacer sticks or dowels to line up large panels before the final press.
- It's still the standard adhesive for laminate countertops decades after laminate itself became a kitchen staple.

## Best Uses

- Rubber-to-rubber and rubber-to-metal bonds
- Laminate and veneer to a solid backing
- Flexible plastics that need a bond that moves with them

## Tips for a Bond That Lasts

- Coat both surfaces and let the solvent flash off until the cement is tacky-dry, not wet, before joining.
- Get alignment right the first time — once contact is made, the bond is essentially permanent.
- Work in a ventilated area; solvent-based contact cements have a strong odor for a reason.
- A rubber-specific formula stays flexible after curing — a general-purpose one can go stiff over time.
