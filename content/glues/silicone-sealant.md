# Silicone Sealant: What It's Good For, and How to Get the Most Out of It

Silicone isn't really trying to be the strongest bond in the room — it's trying to be the most forgiving one, staying flexible for years so the joint can move without cracking.

## Fun Facts & Trivia

- Cured silicone sealant is technically a rubber, formed when the silicone polymer cross-links as it's exposed to moisture in the air — that's why a thick bead can take a surprisingly long time to fully cure through its core.
- It's a mainstay in aquarium construction for exactly the reason it's useful in gluing glass to wood: it flexes with thermal expansion instead of transferring stress into a rigid material.
- '100% silicone' and 'silicone caulk' aren't the same thing — many hardware-store caulks are blended with other polymers and behave differently than pure silicone sealant.

## Best Uses

- Glass-to-wood and glass-to-frame joints that need to flex
- Anywhere long-term water resistance matters more than raw strength
- Bathroom, kitchen, and aquarium sealing

## Tips for a Bond That Lasts

- It won't take paint well — plan the finish before you seal, not after.
- Tool the bead smooth immediately with a wet finger or tool; once it skins over, shaping gets messy.
- Give it the full cure time on the label before exposing it to water — surface-dry isn't the same as cured.
- Clean old silicone residue completely before resealing; new silicone won't bond well to old.
